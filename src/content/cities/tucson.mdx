## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Tucson

- **Verify scope, not just accreditation.** An ISO/IEC 17025 certificate means nothing if the lab's accredited scope doesn't cover your instrument type, measurement range, or parameter. Pull the actual scope document from A2LA's or NVLAP's public directory before you call anyone.
- **Match the lab to your industry's audit standard.** If you're supplying Raytheon or L3Harris — both significant Tucson employers — your calibration certs may need to satisfy AS9100 Rev D requirements, not just ISO 9001. Confirm the lab has aerospace-facing experience, not just general metrology.
- **Ask about NIST-traceable uncertainty budgets.** Any credible lab can produce a calibration certificate. A good one includes the measurement uncertainty at each calibration point, traceable to NIST reference standards. If the lab looks confused when you ask for the uncertainty budget, walk away.
- **Check turnaround against your audit schedule.** Standard turnaround in Tucson typically runs 5–10 business days; rush service (1–3 days) is available at most full-service labs but costs 50–100% more. If you're staring down an AS9100 surveillance audit, don't let a lab's backlog become your nonconformance.
- **Confirm on-site capability for large or fixed equipment.** Not everything comes off the floor. Tucson labs that serve manufacturing and aerospace routinely dispatch metrologists with portable reference standards for dimensional, pressure, and torque calibrations in the field.

> **Pro Tip:** The University of Arizona's engineering and optics programs create a steady pipeline of metrology talent in Tucson — the local labs tend to be technically sharper than the national franchise chains. Ask who will actually be performing the calibration and what their metrology background is.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Tucson typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument complexity, the number of calibration points required, and whether you need a full uncertainty analysis or a pass/fail certificate. A single multimeter calibration might be $75–$150; a full torque wrench set or a dimensional measurement system can run several thousand dollars when you factor in setup, data, and documentation. Most labs require instruments to be clean, functional, and accompanied by the manufacturer's specifications.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote usually means fewer calibration points, no uncertainty data, or an accreditation scope that doesn't actually match your instrument. When your quality auditor pulls the cert and the calibrated range doesn't cover your operating range, you're doing this over — and possibly writing a corrective action. Price it against that outcome.
