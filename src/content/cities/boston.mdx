## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Boston

- **Verify accreditation scope, not just status.** An A2LA or NVLAP certificate tells you a lab is accredited — the *scope document* tells you whether they're actually authorized to calibrate your specific instrument at the range and uncertainty you need. Download it directly from A2LA's or NVLAP's public directory before you even call.
- **Match the lab's industry experience to yours.** A lab that primarily serves machine shops may not have the measurement uncertainty documentation your medical device QMS requires. Ask specifically whether they've supported FDA 21 CFR Part 820 or AS9100 audits.
- **Ask for a sample calibration certificate.** Before committing, request a redacted sample cert. It should include measurement uncertainty (expressed as U at k=2), NIST traceability chain, pass/fail criteria, and the technician's signature. If they balk or it looks thin, walk.
- **Check turnaround against your audit calendar.** Most Boston labs quote 5–10 business days standard; rush service (1–3 days) typically runs a 25–50% premium. If you're six weeks from an AS9100 surveillance audit, confirm capacity in writing — not over the phone.
- **Clarify on-site vs. in-lab calibration.** Some instruments (large CMMs, installed pressure systems) can't travel. Make sure the lab has field calibration capability and that their scope certificate covers on-site work — not all do.

> **Pro Tip:** Massachusetts has a high concentration of defense contractors under ITAR. If your facility handles controlled technical data, ask whether the calibration lab has a documented ITAR compliance policy before letting their technicians on-site.

## What to Expect

Budget $500–$2,500 for a standard calibration engagement covering a handful of instruments with full documentation; complex multi-parameter scopes or tight uncertainty requirements (common in biotech and aerospace) can push $3,000–$5,000. Most labs price per instrument, per parameter range, so a torque wrench costs less than a precision pressure transducer. Standard turnaround is one to two weeks; factor in shipping time if you're sending instruments out.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake is assuming the cheapest quote covers everything you need for your audit. "Calibration" without documented measurement uncertainty, without a NIST-traceable chain, or without pass/fail against your tolerance specs is not audit-ready documentation — it's a sticker on a box. Clarify exactly what's included before you approve a PO.
