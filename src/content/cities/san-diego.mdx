## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in San Diego

- **Verify scope certificates before you talk pricing.** An ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation is only meaningful if the lab's scope document covers your specific parameter, range, and measurement uncertainty requirement. A2LA and NVLAP both publish searchable scope certificates online — look up the lab before you call.
- **Ask about ITAR and controlled access.** San Diego has one of the highest concentrations of defense contractors in the country. If you're working with export-controlled instruments, you need a lab with documented ITAR compliance and physical access controls, not just a good reputation on Google.
- **Match the lab's discipline to your instrument type.** A shop that's excellent at electrical calibration (multimeters, oscilloscopes, power supplies) may have no business touching your torque wrenches or pressure gauges. Confirm the specific CMC (Calibration and Measurement Capability) entries in their scope for your instrument family.
- **Check turnaround commitments in writing.** Standard turnaround in San Diego runs 5–10 business days. Rush service (1–3 days) is available at most labs for a premium — get the SLA documented, especially if you're running against an AS9100 audit window.
- **Ask who actually performs the calibration.** Some labs subcontract disciplines they're not accredited for. If the certificate comes back with a third-party lab's header, your ISO 9001 QA documentation chain just got complicated.

> **Pro Tip:** Cross-reference the lab's A2LA or NVLAP certificate number with the accreditation body's public directory. Certificates expire and can be suspended — a printout on the wall doesn't tell you if it's still active.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in San Diego typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument count, discipline complexity, and whether the lab provides on-site service or requires you to ship. Dimensional and torque calibrations on the lower end; multi-channel data acquisition systems, RF/microwave equipment, and anything requiring special fixturing on the higher end. Most labs provide a calibration certificate with measurement results, uncertainties, and a pass/fail determination against your tolerance spec — budget an extra 2–3 days if you also need an out-of-tolerance notification report for your corrective action log.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake quality teams make is treating calibration as a commodity and awarding solely on price. A $180 certificate from an unaccredited lab is worth exactly nothing when your AS9100 registrar asks for your measurement traceability records. The $400 difference is not a cost — it's the entire point.
