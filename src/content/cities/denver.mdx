## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Denver

- **Verify the accreditation scope certificate, not just the logo.** Any lab can put "ISO/IEC 17025 accredited" on their website. What matters is the A2LA or NVLAP scope document — a public PDF that lists exactly which parameters, measurement ranges, and uncertainty levels the lab is authorized to certify. If your torque wrenches or pressure gauges fall outside that scope, their certificate is decorative.

- **Check the certificate expiration date yourself.** A2LA and NVLAP publish searchable databases. Denver has a handful of labs that have let accreditations lapse between renewals without updating their marketing materials. Thirty seconds on the A2LA directory saves you a rejected calibration record during your AS9100 audit.

- **Match the lab's discipline to your instrument mix.** Denver's aerospace and defense manufacturing base — Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Ball Aerospace — has created deep capacity for dimensional and electrical calibration locally. Thermal and pressure specialists are thinner on the ground. If your facility has a heavy process instrumentation load, confirm depth before assuming availability.

- **Ask about NIST-traceable uncertainty budgets upfront.** A calibration certificate without a stated measurement uncertainty is not compliant with ISO/IEC 17025. If a lab can't produce a sample certificate with expanded uncertainty values before you hire them, walk away.

- **Confirm turnaround against your calibration due-date schedule, not their average.** "Five business days" means nothing if you're running a 200-instrument calibration cycle before a third-party audit. Ask specifically: what's the committed turnaround for your volume, and do they offer on-site calibration for instruments that can't leave the floor?

> **Pro Tip:** Colorado's high altitude (Denver sits at 5,280 feet) affects pressure and atmospheric reference standards. For pressure calibration work, confirm the lab corrects for local atmospheric conditions — it's a detail that bites facilities that assume sea-level correction factors apply.

## What to Expect

Most calibration engagements in Denver run **$500–$5,000**, with per-instrument pricing starting around $50–$150 for standard electrical or dimensional items and climbing steeply for complex electromechanical systems, RF/microwave equipment, or anything requiring specialized fixturing. Expect two to five business days for routine work; expedite fees are real and typically 50–100% above standard rate.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake quality teams make is treating calibration as a commodity line item and selecting purely on per-instrument cost. A low unit price from a lab with a narrow accreditation scope can leave you with certificates that don't satisfy your customer's flow-down requirements — which means you pay twice. Get the scope document first, negotiate price second.
