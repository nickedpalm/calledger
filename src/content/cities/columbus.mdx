## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Columbus

- **Verify accreditation scope before you call.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation sounds universal, but it's parameter-specific. A lab accredited for electrical calibration may have zero authorization for dimensional or pressure work. Pull the scope certificate directly from A2LA's or NVLAP's public directory and confirm the exact measurement ranges cover your instruments before you waste time on a quote.

- **Match the lab to your audit standard.** Columbus has a heavy AS9100 presence thanks to aerospace suppliers clustered around Rickenbacker and the Honda of America supply chain north of the city. If you're an AS9100 shop, ask explicitly whether the lab's calibration certificates call out measurement uncertainty — generic NIST-traceable stickers won't cut it during a third-party audit.

- **Ask for a turnaround commitment in writing.** Verbal "usually about a week" promises evaporate when you're three days from an ISO 9001 surveillance audit. Get the standard and expedite turnaround times on the quote, with any rush fees spelled out.

- **Confirm on-site capability if your equipment can't leave.** Large CMMs, furnaces, torque testers, and fixed production tooling can't be boxed up and shipped. Plenty of Columbus-area labs offer mobile calibration, but their on-site scope is sometimes narrower than their bench scope — confirm the specific parameters they can perform in your facility.

- **Check certificate traceability language carefully.** NIST-traceable means the reference standard chain leads back to NIST. It does not automatically mean the lab performed a measurement uncertainty analysis. For FDA-regulated devices or defense contracts, you need both.

> **Pro Tip:** Ohio requires no state-level laboratory licensing for calibration labs, so accreditation through A2LA, NVLAP, or PJLA is the only meaningful quality signal. If a lab can't produce an active scope certificate, walk.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Columbus typically run $500–5,000 depending on instrument count, parameter complexity, and whether on-site service is involved — a single handheld multimeter calibration sits at the low end, while a multi-instrument torque and pressure package for a manufacturing line hits the top. Standard turnaround is five to ten business days; most labs offer two- to three-day expedite for a 30–50% premium.

> **Reality Check:** Sticker shock on calibration quotes usually means you're comparing a full ISO/IEC 17025 certificate with uncertainty budget to a "traceable" label slapped on by someone with a NIST-purchased reference and no accreditation. That cheaper certificate will get you flagged on the first audit. Price the right thing.
