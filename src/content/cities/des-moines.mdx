## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Des Moines

- **Verify the accreditation scope, not just the logo.** A lab can hang an A2LA certificate on the wall and still be out-of-scope for your specific instruments. Pull the actual scope document from A2LA's or NVLAP's public directory and confirm your equipment type, parameter, and measurement range are listed. If the lab can't send you their current scope certificate in under 24 hours, that's your answer.
- **Ask specifically about NIST traceability chains.** Iowa manufacturers supplying automotive and agricultural equipment OEMs face tight traceability documentation requirements. Ask the lab to walk you through the chain from your instrument back to NIST reference standards — legitimate labs do this without hesitation.
- **Check turnaround against your audit calendar.** Des Moines-area labs serving the manufacturing corridor along I-80 tend to back up in Q1 and Q4 when ISO 9001 and AS9100 renewal cycles peak. If your audit is within 60 days, ask directly about current queue times — not what's posted on the website.
- **Clarify what "calibration" includes.** Some labs issue a certificate; others issue a certificate *plus* an as-found/as-left data report with measurement uncertainty stated in the lab's accredited units. For regulated industries (medical devices, aerospace), you almost always need the latter.
- **Get the certificate before you ship.** For dimensional or torque equipment that requires disassembly or special handling, confirm the lab has the right fixtures and that your instrument will return in the same condition it left — not adjusted to a different specification range than your process requires.

> **Pro Tip:** Iowa's manufacturing base runs heavy on precision agriculture equipment and industrial controls. If your instruments touch hydraulic pressure or load cells, ask whether the lab has on-site deadweight testers or if they're sub-contracting that work — subcontracting isn't disqualifying, but you need to know whose accreditation actually covers that step.

## What to Expect

A typical calibration engagement in Des Moines runs $500–$5,000 depending on instrument count, complexity, and whether you need expedited turnaround. Simple electrical or dimensional tools land at the low end; multi-parameter HVAC instruments, torque wrenches requiring full traceability documentation, or rush jobs with next-day certificates push toward the top. Most accredited labs can turn around standard bench calibration in 3–7 business days; expedited service (24–48 hours) adds a premium of 25–75%.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake is scoping a job as "just calibration" when what you actually need is *calibration plus adjustment plus a new certificate reflecting the adjusted values*. Adjustment is a separate labor step most labs charge for — and if you don't ask upfront, the surprise line item shows up on the invoice after your equipment is already on the bench.
