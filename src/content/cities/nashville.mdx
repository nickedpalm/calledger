## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Nashville

- **Demand the accreditation scope document, not just a certificate.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation means nothing without the scope — a lab might be accredited for electrical but not dimensional, and you won't find that out until after the job. Ask for the current A2LA or NVLAP scope PDF before you quote anything.
- **Match the lab to your audit driver.** AS9100 auditors in the aerospace supply chain around Nashville's Tier 1 suppliers (think the Smyrna Nissan complex and related manufacturers) have different tolerance expectations than ISO 9001 manufacturing audits. Ask whether the lab has experience with your specific standard.
- **Verify NIST traceability chains for your parameter type.** Dimensional, thermal, pressure, and electrical calibrations each have separate traceability chains. A lab that's solid on torque wrenches may not have the reference standards for your pressure transducers. Get specifics.
- **Ask about turnaround before you need it.** Standard turnaround runs 5–10 business days; rush is typically 2–3 days at a premium. If you're on an audit clock — which happens constantly in Nashville's active quality scene — know the lead time before you're scrambling.
- **Check whether they do on-site or lab-only.** Fixed equipment (CMMs, test benches, environmental chambers) can't travel. Many Nashville-area labs offer mobile calibration for on-site work — confirm before scheduling.

> **Pro Tip:** Tennessee has no state-level calibration licensing requirement, which means the accreditation bodies are doing all the quality enforcement. A2LA's online directory at a2la.org lets you verify any lab's current accreditation status in about 30 seconds — do it before you write the PO.

## What to Expect

Most calibration engagements in Nashville run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument type, number of units, and whether you need expedited turnaround or on-site service. A single torque wrench or pressure gauge calibration sits at the low end; a full instrument audit for a medical device manufacturer — dimensional plus thermal plus electrical, with formal uncertainty budgets — lands at the high end.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote is almost never the right answer here. Calibration certificates that won't survive an auditor's scrutiny cost you far more in corrective action, re-calibration, and potential hold on shipments than the $50 you saved. If a lab can't explain its measurement uncertainty statement, that's a red flag, not a bargain.
