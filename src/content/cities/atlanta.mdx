## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Atlanta

- **Verify the accreditation scope, not just the logo.** Any lab can put "ISO/IEC 17025 accredited" on a business card. What matters is the A2LA, NVLAP, or PJLA scope certificate — a publicly searchable PDF that lists the exact parameters, measurement ranges, and uncertainty values the lab is authorized to calibrate. If your torque wrenches or pressure gauges aren't on that list, the calibration isn't accredited no matter what the certificate of calibration says.
- **Match the lab to your audit standard.** Quality teams preparing for AS9100 Rev D audits have different documentation requirements than those going through ISO 9001:2015. Ask specifically whether the lab's calibration certificates include measurement uncertainty statements — auditors increasingly flag certificates that omit this.
- **Check NIST traceability for every parameter.** NIST-traceability isn't a blanket property — it applies parameter by parameter. A lab that's traceable for dimensional work may not be for RF or high-voltage electrical calibration. Get the traceability chain in writing for each instrument type.
- **Ask about on-site calibration capability.** Atlanta's aerospace and manufacturing plants — particularly in the Peachtree City and Newnan corridors — often can't ship precision CMMs or large fixtures. Labs with field calibration teams save significant downtime, especially for pre-audit rushes.
- **Factor in turnaround against your calibration due dates.** Standard turnaround in Atlanta runs 5–10 business days; expedited is typically 24–72 hours at a premium. If you're managing a calibration recall across a production floor, confirm the lab can handle volume without degrading turnaround.

> **Pro Tip:** Pull the lab's A2LA or NVLAP directory listing directly from a2la.org or nist.gov/nvlap before your first engagement. Accreditation can lapse between renewal cycles, and an expired certificate discovered mid-audit is a nonconformance you don't want.

## What to Expect

Most calibration engagements in Atlanta run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument complexity, the number of calibration points required, and whether you need expedited turnaround or on-site service. A single handheld multimeter might run $75–$150; a full dimensional calibration on a coordinate measuring machine or a multi-channel data acquisition system will sit at the high end or beyond.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake is scoping only the instruments you know are due — then getting surprised when the lab's incoming inspection flags additional out-of-tolerance equipment. Budget a 15–20% buffer on any first engagement with a new lab, and ask upfront whether they charge separately for as-found/as-left data or certificate of conformance documentation. Those line items add up fast when your auditor requires them for every instrument in scope.
