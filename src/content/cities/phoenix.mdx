## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Phoenix

- **Verify accreditation scope before anything else.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation is parameter-specific — a lab accredited for electrical calibration isn't automatically authorized for dimensional or pressure work. Pull their A2LA or NVLAP scope certificate at a2la.org or nist.gov/nvlap and confirm the exact measurement parameters and ranges match your instruments.
- **Match the lab to your industry's standard.** Phoenix's aerospace suppliers (and there are hundreds feeding Honeywell, Boeing, and Raytheon operations in the Valley) need AS9100-aware labs that understand flow-down requirements. Medical device manufacturers need labs familiar with FDA 21 CFR Part 820 traceability expectations. General ISO 9001 work has more flexibility, but "general" still means NIST-traceable.
- **Ask for measurement uncertainty statements upfront.** Any accredited lab will provide a calibration certificate with uncertainty values. If a lab balks at discussing measurement uncertainty before you sign a PO, that's a red flag — it usually means they're operating on procedure alone without a real quality system behind it.
- **Confirm turnaround and on-site capability.** Many Phoenix manufacturers can't afford instrument downtime. Ask whether the lab offers mobile calibration for large or fixed equipment, and what their standard turnaround is versus expedited. Some labs serving the I-10 and Loop 303 industrial corridors can turn certificates in 24-48 hours for common electrical and dimensional work.
- **Check for ITAR registration if you're in defense.** A significant slice of Phoenix manufacturing touches controlled technology. If your instruments are used in ITAR-controlled processes, confirm the lab is registered with the State Department's DDTC — not all accredited labs are.

> **Pro Tip:** Don't rely on a vendor's verbal claim of accreditation. Certificate databases are free and take 60 seconds to search. An expired or out-of-scope certificate is worse than no certificate because it gives the false appearance of compliance.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Phoenix typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument type, complexity, and the number of points documented — a single precision torque wrench might be $80–$150, while a full CMM calibration with environmental controls can push well past $2,000. Turnaround on standard items is usually 5–10 business days, with expedited options available for an upcharge of 25–50%.

> **Reality Check:** The most common mistake quality teams make is treating calibration as a per-instrument line item rather than a system cost. If you're sending 40 instruments to four different labs to chase lower per-unit prices, you're paying more in coordination overhead, certificate management, and audit exposure than you'd save. Consolidating to one or two accredited labs with matching scope almost always wins on total cost.
