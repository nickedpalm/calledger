## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Oklahoma City

- **Verify accreditation scope before anything else.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation isn't a blanket endorsement — each lab's A2LA or NVLAP certificate lists the exact parameters and ranges they're authorized to calibrate. A lab accredited for electrical calibration may have zero authorized scope for dimensional or pressure work. Pull the certificate from A2LA's directory at a2la.org or NVLAP's database and match it to your specific instruments before you make a call.

- **Ask for measurement uncertainty statements upfront.** Any lab worth working with will provide a calibration certificate that includes measurement uncertainty (MU) in the as-found and as-left columns. If a lab hedges on this or says "we can discuss that later," walk away. Your ISO 9001 or AS9100 auditor will ask for it.

- **Match the lab to your audit driver.** Tinker AFB suppliers need AS9100-aware labs familiar with defense quality requirements. Oil and gas shops often need pressure and torque traceability. Medical device companies need labs that understand FDA 21 CFR Part 820. Not every ISO/IEC 17025 lab has worked in all three environments — ask for customer references in your specific industry.

- **Check turnaround against your production schedule.** Standard turnaround in this market runs 5–10 business days. If you're up against a surveillance audit or a production line hold, ask explicitly about expedite options — most labs offer 24–72 hour rush service at a premium, but availability varies by discipline.

- **Get a quote that itemizes by instrument type.** Blanket "per instrument" pricing hides a lot. A precision torque wrench and a digital multimeter are not the same calibration job. Itemized quotes let you compare labs honestly and catch scope creep on the back end.

> **Pro Tip:** Oklahoma City is close enough to Tulsa and the DFW metro that several regional labs offer on-site calibration routes through OKC on a scheduled basis. If your instrument list is large or your equipment is immovable, ask whether the lab has a field calibration program — it can cut your turnaround by days and eliminate shipping risk on sensitive equipment.

## What to Expect

Most calibration engagements in Oklahoma City run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument count, discipline complexity, and whether you need expedited certificates. Single-instrument jobs (a pressure gauge, a torque wrench, a handheld DMM) typically land in the $150–$400 range individually, but labs usually require a minimum order. Full instrument packages for a pre-audit sweep of 20–50 instruments are where you hit the $2,000–$5,000 range. Certificates are typically delivered as PDFs within 1–2 business days of calibration completion, with NIST-traceable reference standards documented on the face of each cert.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote almost always means one of two things — no accreditation (just a calibration sticker with no traceable documentation) or stripped certificates that won't satisfy your auditor. If a lab's price is 40% below everyone else's, ask to see a sample certificate before you book. One failed audit costs more than every dollar you saved.
