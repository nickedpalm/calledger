Finding a qualified calibration laboratory in [Charlotte](/locations/north-carolina/charlotte/) shouldn't take three weeks of phone tag and a spreadsheet of accreditation certificates — but for most quality managers, that's exactly what happens. Charlotte's manufacturing corridor stretches from the airport industrial parks out to Concord and Kannapolis, and the labs serving that market range from NVLAP-accredited specialists to generalist shops with questionable scope certificates. This directory cuts through that noise.

## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Charlotte

- **Verify accreditation scope, not just the logo.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation means nothing if the lab's A2LA or NVLAP scope certificate doesn't cover your specific parameter and range. A lab accredited for electrical calibration to 600V isn't automatically qualified to calibrate your 1kV equipment. Download the scope certificate directly from A2LA's public directory at a2la.org — don't accept a PDF emailed by the lab.

- **Match the lab to your audit body.** If you're pursuing AS9100 certification for aerospace work in Charlotte's growing aviation supply chain (Spirit AeroSystems and Honeywell both have regional presence), confirm your lab is already on your registrar's approved list. Some AS9100 auditors will flag labs that carry only ISO 9001 quality systems without 17025 accreditation.

- **Ask for measurement uncertainty budgets upfront.** Any accredited lab should provide a formal uncertainty statement with every certificate. If they can't explain their uncertainty budget before you sign the purchase order, that's your answer.

- **Check turnaround against your production schedule.** Charlotte-area manufacturers running lean inventories often can't afford two-week turnaround. Confirm whether the lab offers on-site calibration for large or fixed equipment — dimensional and pressure tools in particular.

- **Confirm NIST traceability documentation.** Every calibration certificate should include reference to the NIST-traceable standards used. Ask to see a sample certificate before your first engagement. Vague references to "national standards" aren't sufficient for most ISO 9001 or AS9100 audits.

> **Pro Tip:** North Carolina's manufacturing base skews heavily toward pharma (Pfizer has a major facility in Sanford, an hour away) and medical devices. If you're in a regulated industry, ask specifically whether the lab has experience with FDA 21 CFR Part 820 documentation requirements — not all ISO/IEC 17025 labs have calibrated for that audit environment.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Charlotte typically run $500–$5,000 depending on the number of instruments, parameter complexity, and whether you need on-site service or can ship to the lab. Electrical and dimensional instruments on the simpler end of the range often land at $75–$200 per instrument; pressure and temperature calibrations requiring environmental controls or extensive uncertainty documentation push higher. Standard turnaround is 5–10 business days; rush service (1–3 days) typically carries a 25–50% premium.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake quality teams make is shopping on per-instrument price without factoring in certificate format. Some low-cost labs issue one-page summaries that won't satisfy an AS9100 auditor — you'll end up paying twice when your registrar sends findings. Get a sample certificate before you commit.
