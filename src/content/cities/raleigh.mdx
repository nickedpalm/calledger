## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Raleigh

- **Verify accreditation scope before anything else.** "ISO 17025 accredited" is not a binary. A2LA and NVLAP publish searchable scope certificates that list exact parameters (dimensional, electrical, pressure, thermal) and measurement ranges each lab is authorized to calibrate. A lab accredited for torque wrenches is not automatically authorized for your precision pressure gauges. Pull the certificate — it takes two minutes.
- **Match the lab to your audit trail.** If you're AS9100-certified or preparing for an ISO 9001 audit, your calibration records need NIST-traceable uncertainty statements. Ask for a sample certificate of calibration before engaging. If it doesn't include measurement uncertainty (expressed as ± X at k=2), it won't satisfy your auditor.
- **Ask about turnaround time for your specific instruments.** General quotes like "5-7 business days" often apply to simple hand tools. High-end oscilloscopes, CMMs, or torque analyzers may require shipping to a parent facility or scheduling a specialized technician. Get the turnaround in writing for your equipment category.
- **Check for on-site capability.** Several Raleigh-area labs offer mobile calibration for fixed or semi-fixed equipment — CNC machine tool probing systems, large-scale torque equipment, or environmental chambers. On-site service eliminates shipping risk and downtime. It costs more; it's often worth it.
- **Don't ignore PJLA-accredited options.** Perry Johnson Laboratory Accreditation (PJLA) is a fully recognized accreditation body under ILAC mutual recognition. Some quality managers default to A2LA or NVLAP out of habit, but PJLA certs carry equal weight in audits and the lab pool is sometimes less congested for scheduling.

> **Pro Tip:** North Carolina's Research Triangle is home to a dense concentration of pharmaceutical and medical device manufacturers operating under 21 CFR Part 820. If you're in that sector, specifically ask whether the lab's calibration certificates include the traceability language required for FDA-regulated environments — not all ISO 17025 labs format their certs with pharma QA teams in mind.

## What to Expect

A standard calibration engagement in Raleigh runs $500–$5,000 depending on instrument complexity, the number of units, and whether on-site service is required. Simple hand tool cals (torque wrenches, calipers) sit at the low end; multi-channel data loggers, precision electrical standards, or full CMM qualification push toward the top. Most labs turn around bench work in 5–10 business days; rush service (24–48 hours) is available at a 25–50% premium and worth budgeting for post-repair or pre-audit crunch situations.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake quality teams make is sending instruments to a lab without confirming the out-of-tolerance protocol upfront. Some labs charge a full calibration fee even when a unit fails and gets returned uncalibrated. Others include a "found as-received" condition report at no extra charge. Ask before you ship — especially for high-value instruments where the calibration cost is a rounding error compared to repair or replacement decisions riding on that data.
