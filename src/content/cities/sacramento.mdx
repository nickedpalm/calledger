## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Sacramento

- **Verify accreditation scope before anything else.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation isn't a blanket stamp — each lab's certificate specifies exactly which parameters (electrical, dimensional, pressure, thermal) and measurement ranges they're authorized to calibrate. Pull the lab's A2LA or NVLAP scope certificate directly from the accreditation body's public database. A lab that's A2LA-accredited for DC voltage up to 1000V cannot legally provide accredited calibration on your 1500V reference standard.

- **Match the lab to your audit driver.** AS9100 Rev D auditors expect calibration records that trace directly to NIST, with documented measurement uncertainty. If you're on an AS9100 audit cycle — common for Sacramento's aerospace supply chain feeding into McClellan Business Park — confirm the lab issues calibration certificates with uncertainty statements per ILAC P14 guidelines, not just pass/fail stickers.

- **Ask about turnaround for your specific instruments.** General turnaround quotes are meaningless. A lab that handles torque wrenches in two days might take three weeks on a Fluke 5522A multi-product calibrator. Get instrument-specific lead times in writing before committing.

- **Check for on-site calibration capability.** For large equipment, fixed installations, or instruments that drift when transported (certain CMMs, environmental chambers), on-site calibration is the only practical option. Not every accredited lab offers it. Several Sacramento-area labs do — but their on-site scope may be narrower than their bench scope.

- **Don't conflate "calibrated" with "accredited calibration."** A certificate that says "calibrated" without citing an accreditation body and scope number is a repair shop receipt, not a metrologically valid document. Quality auditors know the difference.

> **Pro Tip:** California has no state-level calibration licensing requirement, which means anyone can print a calibration certificate. The only reliable quality signal is third-party accreditation (A2LA, NVLAP, PJLA). If a lab can't provide their current scope certificate on request, walk away.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Sacramento typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument complexity, the number of points calibrated, and whether you need expedited turnaround. A single precision multimeter might run $150–$300; a full annual calibration cycle for a mid-sized machine shop's instrument inventory — calipers, micrometers, torque tools, pressure gauges — lands in the $2,000–$8,000 range. Most labs quote per-instrument pricing and offer volume discounts for blanket POs.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake quality teams make is comparing base calibration cost without accounting for "as-found / as-left" data. Some labs charge extra for the detailed before/after measurement data your ISO 9001 auditor will almost certainly ask for. Get that included in the quote upfront, or budget for the surprise line item.
