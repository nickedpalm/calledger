## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in San Jose

- **Verify accreditation scope before anything else.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation is table stakes, but the scope certificate is what matters. A lab accredited for electrical calibration can't necessarily touch your pressure gauges. Pull the A2LA or NVLAP scope certificate directly from the accreditation body's database — not from the lab's website — and confirm your specific instrument types and measurement ranges are covered.

- **Ask about NIST traceability documentation.** Every calibration certificate should include an unbroken chain of traceability to NIST reference standards. If a lab can't produce clear traceability documentation, their certificates won't hold up during an FDA or DCSA audit. This matters especially for San Jose's dense medical device and defense contractor ecosystem.

- **Match the lab to your industry's audit requirements.** AS9100 Rev D (aerospace) and ISO 13485 (medical devices) have slightly different calibration documentation expectations than plain ISO 9001. Several labs in the South Bay specialize in one vertical but not the other. Ask directly: "Have you supported AS9100 Stage 2 audits?"

- **Check turnaround time against your maintenance schedule.** Standard turnaround in the Bay Area runs 5–10 business days. Expedited service (24–48 hours) is available but typically doubles the cost. If you're running a semiconductor fab or production line that can't wait, confirm rush availability before you ship anything.

- **Get a quote that itemizes by instrument type.** Dimensional, electrical, thermal, and mechanical calibrations are priced differently. A bundled "calibration package" quote can obscure significant per-item variance — and makes it hard to compare labs apples-to-apples.

> **Pro Tip:** California Bureau of Automotive Repair and DTSC environmental rules don't touch calibration labs directly, but if your instruments involve hazardous materials (mercury thermometers, certain pressure media), confirm the lab's handling compliance before shipping. It's a real-world friction point that rarely shows up in lab marketing.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in San Jose typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument count, complexity, and whether on-site service is required. Most quality managers are surprised to find that a single precision CMM calibration can hit $1,500–$2,500 on its own. The process: ship instruments (or schedule on-site), receive calibration certificates with as-found/as-left data and uncertainty values, update your equipment management system. Straightforward.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote almost always means the lab is skipping measurement uncertainty analysis or using out-of-tolerance reference standards. If a certificate doesn't list measurement uncertainty, it's not ISO/IEC 17025-compliant — and your auditor will flag it. Pay for the real thing once instead of explaining a nonconformance twice.
