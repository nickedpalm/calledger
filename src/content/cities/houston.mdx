## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Houston

- **Verify accreditation scope before you call.** ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation isn't a blanket endorsement — each lab's A2LA or NVLAP certificate specifies exact parameters, ranges, and measurement uncertainties it's authorized to cover. A lab accredited for electrical calibration isn't automatically qualified for your torque wrenches. Pull their scope document from the A2LA or NVLAP directory and match it against your equipment list before anything else.

- **Match the lab to your industry's audit standard.** AS9100-registered aerospace suppliers in the Clear Lake and Webster corridors need labs with aerospace-fluent documentation — calibration certificates that explicitly state NIST traceability, measurement uncertainty, and pass/fail criteria. A generalist shop that handles basic ISO 9001 work may not produce paperwork your AS9100 auditor will accept.

- **Ask about on-site service for large or fixed equipment.** Houston's petrochemical plants and fabrication shops along the Ship Channel run equipment that can't be boxed and shipped. Confirm whether the lab sends certified technicians on-site, and whether their mobile calibration capability is covered under the same accredited scope as their bench work — not all labs extend accreditation to field calibrations.

- **Check turnaround time against your shutdown window.** Planned maintenance turnarounds in the energy sector run on brutal schedules. Get a written turnaround commitment, not a verbal estimate. If a lab is vague about lead times in April or October (peak turnaround season), that's your answer.

- **Don't confuse "traceable" with "accredited."** Any shop can stamp "NIST traceable" on a certificate — it's not a protected term. Accreditation by A2LA, NVLAP, or PJLA means a third party has independently verified the lab's measurement processes, equipment, and staff competency. For regulated industries, that distinction matters in an audit room.

> **Pro Tip:** The Texas Department of Agriculture and Texas Commission on Environmental Quality each maintain their own approved calibration requirements for certain measurement categories. If your equipment touches regulatory compliance (weighing systems, emissions monitoring), confirm your lab is on the relevant state-approved list, not just commercially accredited.

## What to Expect

A calibration engagement in Houston typically runs $500–5,000 depending on instrument complexity, the number of points calibrated, and whether the work is bench or on-site. Simple electrical instruments like multimeters and clamp meters sit at the low end; multi-axis dimensional equipment, pressure deadweight testers, or high-accuracy thermal references push toward the top. Standard bench turnaround runs 5–10 business days; rush service (2–3 days) is available at most full-service labs but carries a premium.

> **Reality Check:** "Calibration" and "adjustment" are not the same service, and labs price them differently. A calibration tells you where your instrument sits relative to a reference standard. Adjustment (or "repair and calibrate") corrects it and then calibrates again. If you're budgeting only for calibration and your instruments are out of tolerance, expect a separate quote for the adjustment — and a longer turnaround.
