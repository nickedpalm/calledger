## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Austin

- **Match the lab's accreditation scope to your instruments.** "ISO/IEC 17025 accredited" on a website means nothing until you verify it. Pull the lab's actual scope certificate from the A2LA or NVLAP directory and confirm your specific parameter (dimensional, electrical, pressure, thermal) and measurement range appear. A scope that covers 0–100 psi doesn't help you if your process transmitters run to 3,000 psi.

- **Demand NIST-traceable uncertainty statements.** Any lab worth hiring will provide a calibration certificate that includes measurement uncertainty budgets and a traceable chain back to NIST reference standards. If they can't show you the uncertainty, you don't have a calibration certificate — you have a sticker.

- **Ask who actually does the work.** Austin's calibration market includes mobile on-site services, which matter for large or fixed equipment, and send-in labs, which are faster for small instruments. Several shops subcontract exotic parameters to out-of-state facilities and re-certify the results. Know what you're getting.

- **Check turnaround against your audit timeline.** AS9100 and ISO 9001 auditors in Austin will pull calibration records on-site. If your equipment's due date slipped while it was at a backlogged lab in [Dallas](/locations/texas/dallas/), that's a nonconformance. Ask for a written turnaround SLA before you ship anything.

- **Verify the lab has experience in your industry vertical.** Austin's manufacturing base runs from medical devices (Becton Dickinson, WellDoc) to semiconductor equipment (Applied Materials, Samsung) to aerospace (Cirrus, SpaceX Starbase supply chain). A lab that calibrates torque wrenches for a CNC shop may not be the right fit for Class III medical device CMM verification.

> **Pro Tip:** Texas is home to several A2LA-accredited labs with on-site mobile capabilities. If you're running a production line and can't afford downtime, ask specifically about field calibration — many Austin-area labs will come to you for dimensional and electrical work.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements typically run **$500–$5,000 depending on parameter, quantity, and required uncertainty level** — a handful of handheld DMMs is a line item; a full metrology lab turnover before an AS9100 audit is a project. Turnaround for standard instruments is usually 5–10 business days; expedited service (2–3 days) runs a 25–50% premium, and out-of-tolerance instruments that need adjustment before re-calibration add time.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake is comparing per-instrument quotes without accounting for the certificate format. Some labs quote "calibration" but charge separately for the NIST-traceable certificate, the adjustment, and the out-of-tolerance report. Get a fully loaded quote that includes documentation before you approve a purchase order.
