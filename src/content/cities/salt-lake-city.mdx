## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Salt Lake City

- **Verify the accreditation scope, not just the logo.** An A2LA or NVLAP certificate on a website means nothing if your specific instrument type isn't in their scope of accreditation. Pull the actual scope document from A2LA's online directory or NVLAP's database and confirm the parameter, measurement range, and uncertainty — before you box anything up.
- **Match the lab to your audit standard.** Quality teams prepping for AS9100 Rev D audits need labs whose calibration certificates explicitly reference NIST traceability and state measurement uncertainty in conformance with ILAC guidelines. If the cert doesn't include uncertainty budgets, your auditor will flag it.
- **Ask about on-site capability.** Utah's defense and semiconductor manufacturers often have equipment too large or too sensitive to transport. Several labs along the I-15 corridor offer mobile calibration for dimensional and torque instruments — ask upfront rather than discovering this limitation after scheduling.
- **Check turnaround against your production schedule.** Standard turnaround at most regional labs runs 5–10 business days. Expedited service (1–3 days) is available but commands a 50–100% premium. If you're running a corrective action after a failed audit, factor this into your timeline before committing.
- **Look for labs with aerospace and medical device depth.** Salt Lake City is home to significant aerospace manufacturing (think composite components and avionics supply chain) and a growing medical device sector. Labs that calibrate routinely for these industries understand documentation requirements that general metrology shops don't — things like first-article traceability records and multi-point calibration reports.

> **Pro Tip:** If a lab quotes you a single flat price over the phone without asking about instrument type, quantity, measurement range, or required uncertainty — hang up and call the next one. Legitimate labs gather this information before quoting because it materially affects their cost.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Salt Lake City typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument complexity, the number of calibration points required, and whether you need formal measurement uncertainty analysis documented for audit purposes. A handheld digital multimeter might run $75–$150 as a line item; a precision pressure transducer with a five-point calibration across a specified range starts around $300–$500 per unit, and dimensional metrology on complex tooling can push higher still.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote is rarely the cheapest outcome. Labs that undercut on price often do it by reducing calibration points, skipping out-of-tolerance "as-found" documentation, or issuing certificates that won't survive scrutiny from a third-party auditor. An $80 calibration certificate that fails your AS9100 audit costs you far more than the $150 one that doesn't.
