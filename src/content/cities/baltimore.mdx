## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Baltimore

- **Verify scope before you send equipment.** A2LA and NVLAP publish searchable scope certificates online — don't take a lab's word that they're accredited for your specific parameter and range. A shop accredited for electrical calibration isn't automatically qualified to touch your dimensional gauges.
- **Match the accreditation body to your customer's requirements.** AS9100-regulated defense and aerospace supply chains in Maryland often specify A2LA or NVLAP by name. ISO 9001 shops generally have more flexibility, but confirm with your customer's QMS before you commit.
- **Ask for the measurement uncertainty budget, not just the certificate.** NIST traceability is table stakes. What you actually need is a lab that can demonstrate its uncertainty is within the required 4:1 (or better) ratio for your instrument's tolerance.
- **Check turnaround time against your audit calendar.** Most Baltimore-area labs quote 5–10 business days standard; expedited is 2–3 days at a premium. If you're sourcing new instruments after a repair event, build in extra time for any "as-found / as-left" documentation requirements.
- **Ask if they calibrate on-site.** Several labs serving the Baltimore metro offer mobile calibration for large or fixed equipment — critical for floor-mounted CMMs, environmental chambers, or anything you can't safely ship.

> **Pro Tip:** Maryland doesn't have a state-run calibration accreditation program the way some states do for weights and measures. That means ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation from A2LA, NVLAP, or PJLA is the only meaningful third-party validation. If a lab can't produce a current scope certificate with expiration date, walk away.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Baltimore typically run **$500–$5,000 per visit**, with the wide range driven by parameter type (electrical and dimensional jobs are usually cheaper than complex thermal or pressure calibrations), instrument count, and whether you need expedited turnaround or on-site service. Standard jobs — a handful of calipers, torque wrenches, or multimeters — usually land in the $500–$1,200 band. A full audit-prep sweep of a manufacturing floor can push toward the top of the range.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake quality teams make is shopping on base calibration cost while ignoring the certificate fee. Some labs charge separately for the calibration data package, NIST-traceable certificate, and "as-found" condition report — line items that matter enormously if you're presenting evidence to an auditor. Get an all-in quote, not a rack rate.
