## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Tampa

- **Verify the accreditation scope, not just the certificate.** A lab can be ISO/IEC 17025 accredited for temperature measurement and completely unaccredited for torque. Pull their A2LA or NVLAP scope document (both databases are public) and confirm your specific parameter — dimensional, electrical, pressure, RF, whatever — is explicitly listed with your required range.
- **Match the lab to your audit standard.** AS9100 shops serving Tampa's aerospace corridor (think the suppliers feeding Macdill AFB contracts) need labs with aerospace-specific CMCs on file. ISO 9001 manufacturers in the medical device cluster around USF have different documentation requirements. Don't use a generic "we calibrate everything" shop if your registrar expects parameter-specific uncertainty budgets.
- **Ask for the calibration certificate format before you commit.** A compliant ISO/IEC 17025 certificate must include measurement uncertainty, reference to traceability, and the lab's accreditation number. If they can't show you a sample certificate that hits all three, walk.
- **Check turnaround against your production schedule.** Florida's humidity does a number on precision instruments — thermal and dimensional drift is real. Labs near Tampa's industrial parks off I-4 and I-75 often quote 5–10 business days standard; expedite fees can double your cost if you didn't plan ahead.
- **Confirm NIST traceability chains are current.** Ask when their reference standards were last calibrated and by whom. A lab that calibrates your torque wrenches against a reference that hasn't been sent to a primary lab in three years isn't actually giving you traceability — they're giving you paperwork.

> **Pro Tip:** Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services maintains a Weights and Measures program with its own calibration requirements for commercial weighing devices. If your operation touches anything regulated under Florida Statute §531, confirm your lab understands state-level requirements on top of federal traceability chains.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Tampa typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument complexity, parameter count, and required uncertainty level — a single digital multimeter might cost $75–$150 per unit at volume, while a coordinate measuring machine (CMM) certification with full uncertainty budget documentation can exceed $3,000. Most labs turn around standard work in 5–10 business days, with expedite options (24–72 hours) available at a premium.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake is scoping for "calibration" without specifying accredited vs. non-accredited service. Non-accredited calibration is cheaper and useless for audit purposes — your ISO registrar will flag it immediately. Always specify "ISO/IEC 17025 accredited calibration with certificate" in your PO language, or you may pay twice.
