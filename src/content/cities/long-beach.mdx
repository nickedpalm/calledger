## How to Choose a Calibration Laboratory in Long Beach

- **Verify accreditation scope, not just accreditation status.** Any lab can claim ISO/IEC 17025. What matters is whether their A2LA or NVLAP scope certificate covers your specific instrument type, measurement parameter, and range. A lab accredited for dimensional calibration may have no scope for electrical — always download the current scope doc from the accreditation body's database, not the lab's website.
- **Ask specifically about NIST-traceability documentation.** Your auditor will ask. The calibration certificate needs to cite the reference standard used, its calibration date, and the chain of traceability to NIST. If a lab can't show you a sample cert before you hire them, keep moving.
- **Match the lab to your compliance framework.** If you're chasing AS9100 certification — common for Long Beach's aerospace suppliers feeding into Boeing or SpaceX's Hawthorne facility nearby — you need a lab that understands aerospace first-article requirements, not just general ISO 9001. Ask who their aerospace clients are.
- **Check turnaround against your audit timeline.** Standard turnaround in Southern California runs 5–10 business days; expedited service (usually at 1.5–2x cost) can compress that to 24–48 hours. If you're three weeks from a surveillance audit, don't assume standard will get you there.
- **Get the uncertainty budget in writing.** Measurement uncertainty isn't a footnote — it tells you whether the lab's reference standard is precise enough to meaningfully calibrate your equipment. A lab that resists providing this is waving a red flag.

> **Pro Tip:** The A2LA and NVLAP directories are publicly searchable. Before you call a single lab, pull their scope certificate and confirm the accreditation is current. Labs sometimes let accreditation lapse between renewal cycles and won't volunteer that information.

## What to Expect

Calibration engagements in Long Beach typically run $500–$5,000 depending on instrument count, parameter complexity, and whether you're sending equipment to the lab or scheduling an on-site visit — the latter adds a travel fee but eliminates shipping risk for sensitive instruments like CMMs or torque analyzers. Most labs quote per-instrument, so a single CMC electrical calibration might run $150–$400 while a full dimensional setup with 20 pieces of tooling approaches the top of that range. Turnaround is quoted at time of order, not delivery.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote is almost never the right answer. Labs that significantly undercut market rates are often cutting corners on reference standard maintenance or carrying expired scope certificates. One failed audit finding costs more than the price difference — sometimes by an order of magnitude.
